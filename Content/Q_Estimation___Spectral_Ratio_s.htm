Estimating Q with the spectral ratio method

By comparing the amplitude spectra A 1 and A 2 at two arrival times t 1 and t 2 respectively we can take the ratio of the two

Where Δt is time difference between times

Q is the quality factor related to inverse of attenuation

Taking logarithms of both sides gives


This equation states that the logarithmic ratio of these two amplitudes is a linear function of frequency which is the equation of a straight line.


A linear regression of the left side of the equation versus frequency yields a slope m that is equal to-PiΔt/Q

Therefore;

    Q=-Pi( t 2 - t 1 )/m        (Pi=3.14159.....)

The spectral ratio method is simple in principle, but in practice determining the Q-factor is complicated by overlapping wavelets which lead to amplitude spectra that do not reflect the wavelet spectrum. Also, the linear trend of Y to f is not obvious, and linear fitting is required. More seriously, the spectral ratio method will fail if there are other factors affecting the amplitude spectrum besides attenuation.



The dimension of the anelastic attenuation coefficient α is generally given as dB/unit length or nepers per unit length.

Neper is a unit used to express ratios, such as gain, loss, and relative values.

Note: One neper Np ≡ 20log10e = 8.685889638 dB.


So

Accuracy of estimating Q with spectral ratio's


Because the traveltimes and frequency can be specified with negligible error during the course of analysis, the error in estimating Q can be attributed to the errors in estimating I A 2 (f)/A 1 (f)I·

The power spectra can be estimated in various ways. One way would be to use multiple coherence analysis (White,1973). This separates the signal and noise spectra on the basis of the short-range, trace-to-trace coherence of their spectral components. Alternatively, the random noise in the data could be attenuated by applying a spatial filter such as a Karhunen-Loeve filter or a signal-preserving f-x filter (Harris and White, 1991) to the aligned reflection signal. The alignment of signals is very important because timing jitter would cause a loss of coherent spectral power at high frequencies.

The accuracy depends strongly on the frequency bandwidth of the data. There is also a strong dependence on the  value of Q itself and the time interval over which it is measured. However, implicit in the use of the spectral ratio technique to determine Q is the assumption that the spectrum of the reflection sequence is stationary over the analysis windows. This is a poor assumption for short gates (Hosken in a paper read at the SEG meeting in Houston 1980) and can cause serious errors in the estimates of Q obtained using

The expressions White derived confirm the physical intuition that Q is most easily estimated when the effect of absorption is large.

In practice, information about Q structures is seldom accurate enough to justify laterally varying Q within a layer.

Let us put some representative numerical values into the expressions derived by White..

Let Q=100

         Time window segment: 500ms

         Window separation: 500ms

         Bandwidth: 54Hz (e.g. 8-62Hz)

The time separation (t 2 - t 1 ) must be at least 0.5 s for estimates from the two intervals to be independent.

Then for estimation from surface seismic data the relative standard error in Q is 56%, which is enormous. Doubling the duration of the analysis intervals, and correspondingly their time separation, reduces the relative standard error in Q, which now represents an average over a considerable depth interval, to a tolerable 20%. Doubling the bandwidth brings the same reduction in standard error and is a much more desirable way of improving accuracy if it can be achieved.

Factors that will affect the accuracy of Q estimation

      Multiples

             Multiples are normally higher frequency than the primaries at that traveltime and will therefore bias the frequency decay i.e. the decay may seem less than it really is.

      Noise

             Noise may also bias the apparent frequency decay

      Reflectivity colouring

           We assume that the spectrum in a time window shows the amplitude spectrum of the wavelet. If the reflectivity colour change between the two intervals the spectrum will not be due to the wavelet alone.

       Window sizes

            We need big windows for good averaging and large separations. This means that the time interval over which Q is estimated is large and have poor time resolution.

           The opposite is also true i.e small windows give  good time resolution but poor Q resolution.

4.         Work Break down- Standard work instruction

Workflow break-down to detail what steps are required to perform the role and task.

Estimating Q with the spectral ratio method:

The main steps are;

  1. Agree testing line suitable for the Q-estimation.
  2. Select suitable events of interest along section, avoid significant shallow Q-anomalies such as gas leakage or buried channels.
  3. Define shallow and deep time windows (should be larger than 500ms and separated by at least 500ms)
  4. Calculate amplitude spectra for each window
  5. Divide deep  spectrum by shallow spectrum
  6. Take 20*Log(division)
  7. Estimate gradient to the log of spectral division in a defined frequency range
  8. Calculate Q from estimated gradient

  9. Generate testing QC's defined in Quality Control section

  10. Review with Team Manager and/or AG
  11. Create testing QC summary presentation for external client approval

Data preparation for Q estimation

N.B. Data preparation is a key element to successful analysis.

  • The input data should normally be near stacks or migrations (In Time domain) after demultiple.

Note

  • (Using a near stack eliminates the variation in travel paths, but may also contain more multiple energy which may bias the estimates.)


  • Should have spherical divergence applied through gain or migration. (It is important to note that you can not evaluate the effect of Q compensation without correction for spherical divergence.)
  • The data should have denoise applied. This can be more severe than for conventional processing. Processes such as Cadzow filtering, FX deconvolution or other coherency enhancement methods can be used.
